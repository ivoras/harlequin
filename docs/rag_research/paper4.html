Selecting an embedding model for Harlequin document ingestion: a single-embedder RAG study

Corpus: consolidated Treaty on European Union (CELEX:12016M/TXT). Five candidate embedders, each the sole model on the whole path. Generated from r4_* results.
Abstract. We select a single embedding model for Harlequin document ingestion by evaluating five small open embedders — granite-311M, snowflake-arctic-l-v2.0, Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, embeddinggemma-300M and LFM2.5-Embedding-350M — each run as the only model on the whole path (sentence boundaries, chunk vectors and query vectors), on a 204-page legal corpus with 1152 grounded in-document (incl. 350 exact-extraction) and 201 out-of-domain questions, scored by duplicate-aware, size-normalised cosine retrieval metrics with bootstrap CIs. We report native-prompt vs no-prefix behaviour, sweep the semantic-chunking gate per model, compare eight chunkers and two lexical backends (custom BM25 vs SQLite FTS5) under RRF, and rank models by a pinpoint+rejection composite with a cost table. The recommended triple is snowflake/semadj_g0.3772/fts5_hybrid (dim 1024, recall@1 0.602).

1. Goal & setup

Harlequin ingests documents, chunks them, embeds the chunks into a SQLite + sqlite-vec (cosine) store, and retrieves them for memory and RAG. We pick the single best embedder/chunker/lexical_backend for that path. Each of the five models is served alone on :2235 (llama.cpp, OpenAI API) and does everything — semantic boundaries, chunk vectors and query vectors — so no result mixes models. Chunk budgets are measured in each model's own tokens. Questions (1152 in-document, grounded to exact sentence ids, incl. 350 exact-extraction; 122 misspelled, 100 naive; plus 201 out-of-domain; see §2) are scored duplicate-aware and size-normalised with bootstrap confidence intervals.

2. Evaluation set

The benchmark is 1353 questions over the consolidated TEU: 1152 in-document (grounded to exact sentence ids) and 201 out-of-domain. The in-document set spans natural-language paraphrase, naive and misspelled queries, and 350 exact-extraction questions (30% of in-document) that exercise exact-token retrieval — the regime where dense embeddings are weakest.

Exact-extraction questions are grounded against the PDF, not invented. Mined (250) are anchored on an exact token found in the text — percentages, dates, protocol numbers, article(+paragraph) references, grouped figures. Named-entity (100) were extracted by an LLM (treaty/instrument names, formal Member-State names, institutions & bodies, terms of art, seats, dates) and kept only where the phrase occurs verbatim; grounding is duplicate-aware (acceptable = the support sentence plus occurrences that lexically overlap it). By exact-token kind: article_para 67, article 66, date 37, protocol 37, figure 32, institution 27, country 27, term 25, percent 13, treaty 12, seat 7.

Scoring is unchanged: a hit covers any acceptable sentence; metrics are size-normalised (answer@Btok) with bootstrap CIs; out-of-domain questions test rejection. The exact subset drives §9.1.

3. Naming convention

Every result set is named embedder/chunker/lexical_backend:

  • embedder — one of the five models, in its carried prompt mode (_np suffix marks the no-prefix mode).
  • chunker — the boundary rule and target size (per_sentence, mech_256…mech_1500, overlap_512/1024, sem_adjacent).
  • lexical_backend — the retrieval/fusion arm: dense (sqlite-vec cosine), bm25, fts5, or their dense+lexical RRF hybrids bm25_hybrid and fts5_hybrid.

Example: lfm2/sem_adjacent/bm25_hybrid is the LFM2.5 embedder (native prompts), adjacent-sentence semantic chunks, dense+BM25 RRF.

Embedding models are trained with model-specific instruction prefixes. Symmetric models (granite) prepend nothing; asymmetric retrieval models prepend a query instruction and, for some, a document tag. 6. Semantic-adjacency gate sweep

The sem_adjacent chunker cuts between two sentences when their embedding cosine distance exceeds a gate. The gate's scale is model-specific (drift distributions differ), so it is swept per model at percentiles of that model's adjacent-drift distribution. Finer gates yield smaller chunks and — as everywhere — higher pinpoint scores; we pick each model's gate by the same pinpoint+rejection composite used to rank models, and carry that sem_adjacent variant forward.

6.1 Worked example: how sem_adjacent bundles sentences

The chunker walks the document one sentence at a time, measuring the cosine distance 1−cos(embedding) between each sentence and the next (snowflake embeddings). It keeps appending to the current chunk while that distance stays at or below the gate (0.377 for snowflake), and cuts — starting a new chunk — when the distance exceeds the gate, provided the current chunk already holds at least two sentences (so a single outlier never makes a one-sentence fragment). A token cap also forces a cut. The real run below (consecutive sentences of Article 17 TEU, on the Commission’s functions) shows all three behaviours:

Note s329… stay together because consecutive distances dip at or below the gate (the sentences keep talking about the same thing), while a jump above the gate ends the chunk. For reference, adjacent distances across the whole corpus have median 0.53 (p25 0.41, p75 0.70), so the low gate makes most boundaries cut — chunks are small, which §6.2 quantifies.

7. Chunker comparison

Eight chunkers per model, dense retrieval, carried prompt mode: per-sentence, four mechanical token caps, two overlapping caps, and the model's selected sem_adjacent. Smaller chunks win on pinpoint metrics and OOD rejection; larger caps trade that for deep recall. answer@B compares at an equal retrieved-token budget.

How a chunk is indexed. Every chunker emits the same kind of object: a contiguous run of whole sentences that becomes one atomic record, keyed by a shared chunk id across all three structures — the chunks table (text + sentence-span provenance), the sqlite-vec vec0 index (one cosine vector), and an FTS5 row (a single text field, rowid = chunk id). The chunker decides only what a record is — where the boundaries fall — while that same record is what dense, BM25 and FTS5 each retrieve; because the id is shared, their ranked lists fuse directly by id under RRF (§8). A chunker therefore affects retrieval only by changing record boundaries and sizes, which is exactly what the table below isolates.

Why one global weight is wrong. FTS5/BM25 is the right tool for exact-token queries (article numbers, percentages, dates, defined terms of art) that dense embedders blur — e.g. ‘Article 50’ vs ‘Article 52’ — but it adds noise on paraphrase. This eval set is 70% paraphrase / 30% exact-extraction (§2), so the ‘clean’ column above mixes both regimes and the aggregate weight preference is a compromise. The right reading is ‘do not over-weight FTS5 globally, and never drop it’. §9.1 separates the two regimes on a dedicated exact-extraction probe and shows the weight preference inverts — motivating per-query score-gated fusion instead of a fixed weight.

9.1 Exact-extraction probe and score-gated fusion

We mine the corpus for 134 grounded queries anchored on an exact token dense embeddings blur — percentages (e.g. the 55 % / 65 % qualified-majority thresholds), protocol numbers and article+paragraph references — and score them against the 680 clean paraphrase questions. The weight preference inverts: exact-extraction wants the FTS5 arm up-weighted (dense alone is weakest; recall@1 climbs steeply with FTS5 weight), while paraphrase wants it down-weighted — one global weight cannot serve both.

Score-gating beats a flat weight. Keeping only the top-decile FTS5 hits per query (the confident, rare-token matches) and then weighting them lets each query decide whether lexical evidence is trustworthy, instead of applying one global weight. At equal weight, gated p90 (w=1) keeps most of the exact-extraction gain while holding paraphrase near the dense level — the balanced operating point. Up-weighting the survivors, gated p90 (w=2) gives the best exact-extraction recall@5 in the table, at a clear paraphrase-precision cost — the right pick when exact-token queries dominate. Harlequin uses the gated p90 fusion for document search (FTS5 arm gated to its top decile) with the FTS5 weight moderated toward 1 (default 1.5) — between the balanced (w=1) and exact-maximising (w=2) points above — to keep most of the exact-extraction gain without paying the full paraphrase cost. The same per-query top-decile gate is what documents.Search implements.

11. Generalization

Replication of the winner (snowflake-arctic-embed-l-v2.0-q8_0.gguf, one model the whole path) on 120 grounded questions over a contrasting non-legal corpus (Darwin, Origin of Species). The same size law and semantic≥random ordering hold.

12. Conclusion

For Harlequin document ingestion, adopt snowflake/semadj_g0.3772/fts5_hybrid: it leads the pinpoint+rejection composite at dimension 1024. Index small, semantically- or mechanically-bounded chunks, and keep a dense+lexical fusion: with a realistic 30% exact-extraction load (§2), the winner’s lexical arm is fts5_hybrid — the FTS5 hybrid earns its place on article numbers, figures and named entities, beating dense alone. A fixed global FTS5 weight is a compromise (§9); score-gated fusion (drop low-BM25 hits, up-weight the confident survivors, §9.1) is the recommended production scheme. BM25 and SQLite FTS5 are interchangeable, so FTS5 is the dependency-free choice. Prompt mode is per the carried decisions: snowflake-arctic-l-v2.0, Qwen3-Emb-0.6B do best with their native query prefix; embeddinggemma-300M, LFM2.5-Emb-350M on raw text.
