Chunking and retrieval for RAG over legal text: a size-controlled study

Corpus: consolidated Treaty on European Union (CELEX:12016M/TXT). Embedders: granite-embedding-311M, Qwen3-Embedding-4B. Generated from r2_* results.
Abstract. We study how chunk granularity, boundary placement, embedding model and retrieval method affect dense-retrieval RAG on a 204-page legal corpus, evaluated with 802 grounded in-document questions and 201 out-of-domain questions using duplicate-aware, size-normalised metrics with bootstrap confidence intervals. Three results. (1) Chunk size is the primary axis: recall@1 varies monotonically with mean chunk size within every boundary-placement rule. (2) Controlling for size, semantic-adjacency boundaries give a small but consistent gain over random boundaries (+0.04–0.05 recall@1, and +0.07–0.08 on a contrasting corpus) in the small-chunk regime. (3) Absolute quality is set by the embedder and chunk granularity, not bolt-on LLM stages: a stronger instruction embedder with structural chunking raises recall@1 from 0.42 (a fixed-cap dense baseline) to 0.64, and a BM25 hybrid further raises recall@5, whereas HyDE, contextual-prefix embedding and zero-shot LLM reranking did not help. End-to-end answer correctness rises from 0.61 to 0.68 (LLM judge). We report the full grid and a generalization check on a contrasting non-legal corpus.

1. Introduction

Chunking-rule comparisons are confounded by chunk size: smaller chunks mechanically score higher on point metrics. We run a size-controlled comparison of boundary-placement rules (semantic, structural, fixed, random) and, separately, measure the retrieval-stack levers that actually move absolute quality (embedding model, lexical hybrid, reranking, query expansion, contextual embedding). The thesis under test is that adjacent-sentence semantic chunking is worth its complexity once size is controlled, and we quantify exactly how much it adds relative to the dominant factors.

2. Setup

  • Corpus: consolidated TEU, 2 112 sentences with page/line provenance.
  • Embedders: granite-embedding-311M (768-d) and Qwen3-Embedding-4B (2560-d, with query instruction prefix), both via llama.cpp; chunk budgets in model tokens.
  • Store: SQLite + sqlite-vec (cosine). Lexical: Okapi BM25. Fusion: RRF. Rerank/HyDE/context/judge: Qwen3.6-35B chat.
  • Questions: 802 in-document (grounded to exact sentence ids; 122 misspelled, 100 naive) + 201 out-of-domain. Scoring: a hit covers any duplicate-aware acceptable sentence; metrics carry 95% bootstrap CIs; answer@B evaluates at an equal retrieved-token budget B to neutralise the size confound.

5. Embedding model and retrieval stack

Absolute quality is driven by the embedder and lexical fusion. The stronger embedder helps structural/mechanical chunks most (and, with last-token instruction pooling, underperforms on single-sentence chunks); BM25 hybrid lifts recall@5 broadly; the best static pipeline is qwen4b/structure_1024/dense at recall@1 0.643.

5.1 Techniques that did not help

Three popular add-ons gave no benefit on this corpus and embedder. HyDE query expansion was neutral-to-negative (it only helped structural chunks, and hurt fine-grained ones). Contextual Retrieval (an LLM-written context prefix per chunk) was neutral: the added tokens dilute a small chunk's embedding and the bi-encoder is not instruction-tuned to exploit them. Zero-shot LLM listwise reranking with a general chat model (reasoning disabled for tractability) actively degraded a strong bi-encoder ordering (Table 3) — a dedicated cross-encoder, not a prompted generator, is the right tool. The lesson: spend effort on the embedder and lexical hybrid, not on bolt-on LLM stages.

5.2 Lexical backend: SQLite FTS5 vs custom BM25

We compare two lexical arms: a custom numpy BM25 and SQLite’s built-in FTS5 (its own BM25 and tokenizer). They are within noise on recall@1/@5 both standalone and inside the dense hybrid (FTS5 is marginally lower on large chunks, where its default tokenizer keeps stopwords), so FTS5 is a dependency-free drop-in lexical backend.

5.3 End-to-end answer correctness

An LLM judge scored whether the top-5 retrieved context answers the question. The best static pipeline reaches 0.68 vs 0.61 for the fixed-cap baseline: retrieval gains carry through to generation.

6. Generalization

Replication on 120 grounded questions over a contrasting non-legal corpus (Darwin, Origin of Species): the same size law and semantic>random ordering hold.

7. Discussion

Two levers dominate. Size sets point precision and false-positive separation (small) versus deep recall and context (large); the answer@B view shows small chunks win at equal context budget. Embedder + lexical hybrid + reranking set the absolute ceiling. Boundary placement is third-order: semantic-adjacency adds a small, size-controlled gain over arbitrary cuts and is the best content-blind-free option when no document structure exists, but structural boundaries plus a strong embedder are the simplest strong baseline here.

8. Conclusion

For RAG over structured legal text: index small, semantically- or structurally-bounded chunks; pair a strong instruction embedder with BM25 fusion and a reranker; choose chunk size by the downstream context budget. Semantic-adjacency chunking earns a modest, quantified place once size is controlled — it is not, by itself, the main driver of retrieval quality.
