Sentence-level semantic chunking for retrieval over legal text: an empirical comparison

Embedding-RAG study · corpus: Consolidated Treaty on European Union (EUR-Lex CELEX:12016M/TXT) · generated from eval.py results

Abstract. We compare chunking strategies for dense-retrieval RAG on a 204-page legal corpus, embedding with a 768-dim multilingual model (granite-embedding-311M) and indexing in SQLite via the sqlite-vec extension. The central question is whether adjacent-sentence semantic chunking (sem_adjacent: split where consecutive sentence embeddings diverge) is competitive with per-sentence, structure-aware, mechanical (fixed token cap), overlapping, and cumulative-drift chunking. We evaluate 19 index variants against 802 grounded in-document questions and 201 out-of-domain questions, scoring exact answer hits via sentence-id containment, answer localisation, and answerable/unanswerable separability. sem_adjacent at gate 0.12 reaches recall@1 0.50 and the best misspelled-query robustness (0.87) of any method, second only to per-sentence chunking on rank-1 accuracy and false-positive rejection while producing coherent multi-sentence chunks. Chunk size is the dominant factor: smaller chunks improve precision, localisation, and false-positive rejection; larger chunks improve deep recall. Cumulative-embedding drift chunking provides no benefit over a fixed token cap.

1. Introduction

Retrieval quality in RAG depends heavily on how a document is split into embedded units. We test the thesis that splitting on local semantic boundaries — cutting where two consecutive sentences diverge in embedding space (sem_adjacent) — yields better retrieval than both size-driven splitting (fixed token caps) and maximally fine splitting (per-sentence), without per-sentence fragmentation.

We position sem_adjacent against five families: per-sentence, structure-aware (split on legal headings), mechanical (token caps 256–1500), overlapping mechanical, and cumulative-drift (“successive”) chunking, plus a centroid-based semantic variant. All are evaluated on the same grounded question set with identical metrics.

2. Experimental setup

Corpus. Consolidated Treaty on European Union (TEU), 204 pages, extracted to 2 112 sentences with page/line provenance. Content spans the treaty articles, 37 protocols (Court of Justice, ECB/ESCB and EIB statutes, Schengen, euro opt-outs) and annexed declarations.

Embedding & storage. granite-embedding-311M-multilingual-r2 served by llama.cpp (OpenAI-compatible API); 768-dim, L2-normalised, 1500-token context. Vectors are stored one-database-per-index in SQLite using sqlite-vec (vec0 virtual table, distance_metric=cosine); KNN runs as embedding MATCH ? AND k=?. Chunk token budgets are measured in the model’s own tokeniser so caps map onto the 1500-token limit.

Evaluation set. 802 in-document questions authored from the source and grounded to the exact supporting sentence id (hence page/line); 201 out-of-domain questions (far off-topic plus hard near-domain EU topics absent from this document). 122 questions are misspelled and 100 are deliberately naive (“dumb”) to probe query-quality robustness; coverage spans 138 pages.

3. Chunking methods

Every chunk is a contiguous run of whole sentences (sentences are never split), so a chunk carries the sentence-id span it covers. This makes scoring exact and independent of the chunker.

4. Metrics

  • recall@k (k=1,3,5,10): fraction of in-document questions whose top-k results contain a chunk whose sentence span includes the supporting sentence.
  • MRR@10: mean reciprocal rank of the first hit.
  • Localisation (loc tok): mean token size of the first hit chunk — lower means the answer is pinned to a tighter span.
  • Out-of-domain separability: top-1 cosine as a confidence score; reported as ROC AUC and equal-error rate (EER) between answerable and unanswerable queries.
  • Subgroup recall@5 for misspelled and naive questions.

5. Results

5.1 Chunk size is the dominant axis

Sweeping the mechanical token cap isolates size from strategy. Precision (recall@1) and out-of-domain AUC fall as the cap grows, while deep recall (recall@10) rises — a precision/coverage trade-off (Figure 2). Across all 19 variants, recall@1 tracks mean chunk size monotonically (Figure 3): the small-chunk methods (per-sentence, sem_adjacent_g0.12, structure, mech_256) form the high-precision cluster.

5.2 The semantic gate controls the precision/size trade-off

The sem_adjacent gate is an effective size control: raising it from 0.12 to 0.18 grows chunks (689→473→297) and lowers recall@1 (Figure 4). The tightest gate is the strongest operating point here. The centroid variant is more conservative (centroid drift is smaller) and behaves between mechanical and adjacent splitting.

5.3 Cumulative-drift chunking

The cumulative-drift (“successive”) family is statistically indistinguishable from a fixed 1024-token cap (Table 3): the cumulative chunk embedding moves negligibly as sentences are appended, so the size cap, not the gate, determines the cut. Drift measured between adjacent sentences, by contrast, scales with topic change and drives the sem_adjacent result above.

6. Discussion

Two levers explain the field. First, chunk size: a smaller chunk’s mean-pooled embedding is dominated by the answer rather than diluted by unrelated sentences, improving rank-1 accuracy, localisation and false-positive rejection; larger chunks each cover more text and so help only deep recall. Second, where the boundaries fall: sem_adjacent and structure-aware splitting both place boundaries at meaning/structure shifts and so beat size-matched mechanical chunking. sem_adjacent additionally needs no document structure, and is the most robust to misspelled and naive queries, making it the preferred general method when no reliable headings exist.

Operationally: index small, semantically-bounded chunks; expand a hit to its parent span at read time if the generator needs context; apply a cosine threshold (~95% true-positive operating point) to reject out-of-domain queries, whose residual errors are near-domain.

7. Conclusion

Adjacent-sentence semantic chunking is competitive with the strongest baselines on legal-text retrieval: second-best rank-1 accuracy, best robustness to poor queries, strong false-positive rejection, and coherent multi-sentence chunks. Chunk size is the dominant factor, and semantic boundaries beat size-matched mechanical ones. Cumulative-embedding drift offers no advantage over a fixed token cap.
